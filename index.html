How Earthquake Develop?

Earthquakes develop when stress builds up along tectonic plate boundaries‚Äîdivergent, convergent, or transform‚Äîuntil the pressure overcomes friction, causing a sudden slip. This releases energy as seismic waves, including fast P-waves, slower S-waves, and destructive surface waves like Love and Rayleigh waves.

Earthquakes are sudden ground movements caused by shifts in Earth's crust, often due to tectonic plate activity. While most are too small to feel, powerful quakes can trigger tsunamis, landslides, and structural collapses, posing serious risks in populated areas. Learn more about these unpredictable Earth tremorsand what to do if one rattles near you.

Major quakes often start at a fault's nucleation zone, with foreshocks sometimes preceding the main event. Aftershocks follow as the crust adjusts. Some earthquakes, like megathrust quakes at subduction zones, can exceed magnitude 9, while others are triggered by volcanic activity or human actions like mining or fluid injection.

How to Measure Earthquakes

Earthquakes are measured to determine both their strength and impact on people and structures, using two main parameters: magnitude and intensity.

Magnitude

Magnitude is a quantitative measure of the energy released at the earthquake's source. Seismographs record seismic waves. The Moment Magnitude Scale (Mw) is the most accurate scale for all sizes of earthquakes.

Intensity

Intensity describes the observed effects at specific locations, including impacts on people, buildings, and the environment. The Modified Mercalli Intensity (MMI) scale ranges from imperceptible tremors to catastrophic damage.

Importance

  • Guides structural engineering and safe building design.
  • Helps urban planners identify high-risk areas.
  • Supports emergency preparedness and early warning systems.
  • Provides scientific data for research and disaster mitigation.

Seismic Zones

Plate Boundaries Ring of Fire Intraplate Zones Volcanic Regions

Plate Boundaries ‚Äî Most earthquakes cluster where tectonic plates meet:

  • Convergent: Powerful quakes at subduction and collision zones.
  • Divergent: Moderate events along oceanic ridges.
  • Transform: Shallow quakes along strike-slip faults, like San Andreas.
Key idea: Stress builds at boundaries until sudden slip releases energy as seismic waves.

Global Concentrations ‚Äî Notable seismic belts include:

  • Pacific Ring of Fire: Most active belt with frequent quakes and volcanoes.
  • Alpine‚ÄìHimalayan Belt: Stretches from Mediterranean to Himalayas.
  • Intraplate Earthquakes: Occur inside plates on ancient fault lines.
  • Volcanic Areas: Quakes caused by magma movement underground.
Remember: Earthquakes can also occur far from plate edges due to crustal weaknesses.

Before an Earthquake - Preparation

Create an Emergency Plan

  • Identify safe spots in each room (under sturdy tables, away from windows).
  • Plan evacuation routes and meeting points.
  • Teach family members how to "Drop, Cover, and Hold On".

Prepare an Emergency Kit

  • Water (at least 3 days' supply).
  • First-aid kit, flashlight, extra batteries.
  • Whistle, dust mask, personal documents, small cash.

Strengthen Your Home

  • Secure heavy furniture and appliances to walls.
  • Store breakable items in low, closed cabinets.
  • Check for structural weaknesses in your building.

Stay Informed

  • Know local earthquake risks.
  • Keep emergency contact numbers handy.
  • Follow local disaster management guidelines.

During an Earthquake - Safety Actions

Indoors

  • Drop to the ground, cover your head/neck under sturdy furniture, and hold on.
  • Stay away from glass, mirrors, and heavy objects.
  • Do not run outside during shaking.

Outdoors

  • Move to an open area away from buildings, power lines, and trees.
  • Stay put until shaking stops.

In a Vehicle

  • Stop in a safe area away from bridges, tunnels, and overpasses.
  • Stay inside until the shaking stops.

After an Earthquake - Recovery

Check for Injuries & Hazards

  • Give first aid if needed.
  • Be careful of broken glass, gas leaks, and downed power lines.

Expect Aftershocks

  • Stay alert and prepared to "Drop, Cover, and Hold On" again.

Communicate Safely

  • Use text messages or social media to contact family.
  • Keep phone lines free for emergencies.

Inspect Your Home

  • Avoid entering severely damaged buildings.
  • Turn off gas, water, and electricity if you suspect leaks or damage.

Follow Official Instructions

  • Listen to the radio, TV, or trusted online sources for updates.
  • Only return home when authorities say it's safe.
